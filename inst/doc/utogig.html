Using thermodynamics to extract geochemical information from genomes (2022)

This vignette runs the code to make the plots from the following paper:

Dick JM, Boyer GM, Canovas PA III, Shock EL. 2022. Using thermodynamics to extract geochemical information from genomes. Submitted manuscript.

This vignette was compiled on 2022-07-04 with JMDplots 1.2.13.

library(JMDplots)

Chemical adaptation to redox conditions in the reference proteomes of methanogens (Figure 1)

utegig1()

Data sources: Hierarchical clustering tree of methanogenesis genes (Lyu and Lu, 2018); ZC calculated for reference proteomes (Dick and Tan, 2022) based on RefSeq protein sequences (OLeary et al., 2016). See Table 1 in the paper for references for optimal growth temperature (Topt).

Relative stabilities of organic compounds depend on redox conditions (Figure 2)

utegig2()

Data sources: List of organic compounds from Shock and Canovas (2010) and TCA cycle metabolites from Canovas and Shock (2016). See Table 2 in the paper for references for thermodynamic data.

Thermodynamic model for methanogen niche partitioning (Figure 3)

utegig3()

Data sources: Lost City T and H2 concentration (Kelley et al., 2005), Rainbow vent fluid mixing model (Shock and Canovas, 2010), methanogenic and non-methanogenic sediments (Lovley and Goodwin, 1988).

Chemical and thermodynamic analysis of evolutionary divergence along redox gradients (Figure 4)

utegig4()

Data sources: Methanogens (Lyu and Lu, 2018), Nif-bearing organisms (Poudel et al., 2018), Thaumarchaeota (Ren et al., 2019). Amino acid compositions are from RefSeq proteomes (methanogens and Nif-bearing organisms) or NCBI and IMG genomes (Thaumarchaeota).

ZC of proteins and lipids in hot springs (Figure S1)

utegigS1()

Data sources: Proteins (Dick and Shock, 2013) and lipids (Boyer et al., 2020).

Intermediate logaH2 calculated for different compound classes (Figure S2)
